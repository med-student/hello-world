Insight

What we do

Our Focus

Our research is focused on dysphagia (i.e., swallowing impairment), which can be caused by a variety of neurological disorders ranging from fatal degenerative diseases (e.g., amyotrophic lateral sclerosis or ALS) to surgically-induced (iatrogenic) laryngeal nerve injury that can occur during anterior neck procedures such as thyroidectomy and cervical spine surgery. Dysphagia in these conditions significantly contributes to a poor quality of life, major depression, decreased general health, and increased risk of life-threatening aspiration pneumonia. Current treatments are predominantly palliative rather than curative because the underlying pathological mechanisms that cause dysphagia remain largely unknown for each condition. Our research addresses this major clinical problem by studying mice, which have a compressed life cycle (~2 years) that permits longitudinal investigations throughout the disease course and across the life-span to accelerate the scientific discovery process. While numerous mouse models of neurological conditions currently exist, they have rarely been studied relative to dysphagia. This lack of attention is surprising, given that dysphagia is a serious consequence of most neurological disorders.

Goals

  1. Establish mouse models of dysphagia for a variety of neurological disorders, thereby strengthening the translational potential of these models to ultimately benefit humans.
  2. Elucidate the mechanisms underlying dysphagia, which are likely different for each neurological disorder.
  3. Develop novel, effective treatments for dysphagia in mouse models of neurological disorders to implement in clinical trials with patients.

What we have accomplished

  • Successfully adapted the two gold-standard clinical diagnostic tests for dysphagia (i.e., videofluoroscopy and endoscopy) to permit longitudinal investigations in mice
  • Developed miniaturized test equipment and novel test protocols for fluoroscopic and endoscopic evaluations of swallowing in mice
  • Establishment and validation of mouse models (i.e. ALS, serotonin deficiency, iatrogenic laryngeal nerve injury, and primary aging) which is currently being studied to elucidate the underlying pathological mechanisms contributing to dysphagia
  • Exploration of candidate treatment strategies to delay, slow, or reverse the underlying neuropathology of dysphagia
